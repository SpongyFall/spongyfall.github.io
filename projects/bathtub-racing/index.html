Project Overview

What This Is

Southern Bathtub Race is a game commemorating the old SPSU bathtub races, where students raced custom bathtub karts around campus from 1968 to 1991. A previous senior project team built the original single-player version, and our team was assigned to continue it.

Our sponsor created a multiplayer SDK called GONet, and our job was to turn the project into an online multiplayer game: Steam lobbies, Steam relay, GONet session startup, synced karts and AI, race flow, and a working offline mode.

I worked in Unity with one teammate while the other two focused on documentation, presentation material, and design docs. In practice, I owned most of the design and implementation work, especially the Steamworks, GONet, lobby, race-flow, and sync systems.

C-Day Showcase

1st Place Undergraduate Project

We presented the project at KSU's C-Day Computing Showcase and won first place out of roughly 80+ projects. It was a strong proof point that our project was more than a class assignment: we had a stable playable build, working multiplayer flow, and clear story for why the game mattered.

My Role

What I Built

Steam Lobby Flow

I integrated Steamworks and built the lobby flow: hosting, joining, random matchmaking, lobby codes, player ordering, lobby metadata, and the handoff from Steam lobby state into a GONet race session.

GONet Multiplayer

I wired GONet into the project and built the multiplayer sync around it: player kart ownership, deterministic spawn placement, race start coordination, AI sync, race data, disconnect handling, and cleanup between sessions.

Game Loop Polish

We finished early, so I started adding out-of-scope polish: offline mode, track selection, lap configuration, end-game flow, controller support, UI navigation, customization save/load, and bug fixes from the original build.

Technical Work

The Hard Part

The hard part was not just "add multiplayer." It was getting Steam, GONet, Unity scenes, UI, and race logic to agree on the same state. Steam handled social lobby presence, while GONet handled the actual race session. The lobby had to know when the host started, clients had to connect to the right Steam relay target, and the race could not start until every player was spawned correctly.

I also fixed race logic that multiplayer exposed immediately: lap counting, waypoint tracking, player placement, track selection, race start timing, ownership, desyncs, and lobby state inconsistencies. A lot of the project became finding the exact place where UI, networking, and race state disagreed.
